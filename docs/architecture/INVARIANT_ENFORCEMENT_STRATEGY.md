## 1. Why this doctrine exists

The consolidation phase closed 2026-05-10. Across six audit rounds plus ten archived cross-reviews of architectural pressure, the question shifted from "what architecture should exist?" to "which stabilized truths deserve mechanized enforcement?" The five-tier doc-authority hierarchy (Doctrine / ADR / Spec / PLAN / STATUS) answered the first question; this doctrine answers the second.

The shift is binding. Phase 1 is **Executable Governance Architecture** — selectively codify stabilized truths into mechanized enforcement, document graduation criteria, distinguish enforceable invariants from intentionally-unenforced doctrine. Phase 2 (reflection / tooling) and Phase 3 (GPU abstraction) are downstream and must not bleed in. Carrying consolidation-phase reflexes — synchronize / propagate / reconcile / verify — would degrade Phase 1 into "Round 10 cleanup with tests"; that outcome is failure. The mode-shift is itself part of the discipline.

This doctrine is the **answer to enforcement-graduation philosophy**, not a roadmap of next graduations. It says where the boundary between "prose holds the truth" and "code refuses the violation" should sit, and — critically — why much of the workspace's architectural truth should remain on the prose side of that boundary indefinitely. The graduation question is itself a senior-engineering act: the largest near-term governance risk is over-codification, not under-enforcement. The asymmetry of this document — substantially more weight on costs of mechanization, on system-level failure modes, and on doctrine-that-must-stay-prose than on graduation triggers — encodes that risk-priority structurally.

The cross-review-#10 framing (archived in `change.md` 2026-05-10 09:50) names what the consolidation phase produced: *"architectural pressure repeatedly failed to force premature abstraction expansion."* Phase 1 reintroduces that pressure under a new disguise — mechanization-of-doctrine — and the doctrine here is the response. It is the workspace's first deliberate attempt to reason about *when not to codify* with the same care previously applied to *what to design*.

## 2. Vocabulary — concept distinctions

Six terms recur across this doctrine and the broader Phase 1 work. Their conflation is the most common failure mode in governance discussions: an "important" invariant is assumed to demand enforcement; an "advisory" claim is assumed to be optional rather than deliberate; "verification" is conflated with "enforcement" because both run in CI. The distinctions below are load-bearing for every other section.

| Concept | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Invariant** | An architectural truth claim. The thing the doctrine asserts is, and ought to remain, true. Examples: "renderer never owns authoritative geometry"; "PIE round-trips are byte-identical"; "Layer 4 cannot mutate Layer 1." |
| **Enforcement** | A mechanized verification mechanism — compiler refusal, lint failure, runtime assertion, test failure. Enforcement is what the workspace builds *for* an invariant; it is not the invariant itself. An invariant exists whether or not it is enforced. |
| **Escalation** | Movement between enforcement tiers. Prose → linting; linting → executable; warning-tier → error-tier. Also includes **demotion** (enforcement → prose) when codification is found to have ossified the wrong shape. Escalation is bidirectional in principle and asymmetrically expensive in practice. |
| **Authority surface** | Where the invariant is normative — the place the truth claim *originates*. The doctrine doc, the ADR, the spec section. The authority surface answers "where do I go to learn that this is true?" |
| **Verification surface** | Where the invariant is tested or checked — the lint module, the integration test, the runtime assertion. Multiple verification surfaces may share a single authority surface; the inverse is rare and usually a smell. The verification surface answers "how do I confirm the truth holds?" |
| **Advisory guidance** | Intentionally non-mechanized doctrine. **Not** "unenforced because we haven't gotten around to it"; non-enforced *by design*, because mechanization would mistate the truth or ossify the substrate. The §9 doctrine-that-should-remain-prose list is the worked example. |

The most important non-equivalence: **importance ≠ executability**. An invariant can be load-bearing for the workspace and remain prose forever. Conversely, an invariant can be enforced without being significant — graduating something trivial because the codification was easy is a variant of premature mechanization. Vocabulary discipline prevents this conflation from quietly degrading the §5 graduation criteria into "did we have time to write a lint?"

The authority-vs-verification distinction is similarly load-bearing. A passing lint is a verification result, not an authority statement; the lint *checks* an invariant whose authority lives in the doctrine. Treating the lint as the authority surface (§6.3 false authority) is the single most common form this confusion takes.

## 3. Invariant-class taxonomy

Workspace invariants partition into seven classes by the kind of authority they assert. The reasoning per row IS the deliverable; the table form is convenient but the row-by-row WHY is what graduates a candidate, not the row label.

- **Structural authority boundary.** "X owns this; Y must not own this." Examples: cad-graph owns canonical geometry; the renderer NEVER owns authoritative geometry (`SCENE_EXTRACTION_CONTRACT.md` §3.3); editor-state coordinates, never authors. These are dependency-direction rules — typically enforceable at the import / dependency-graph level because the violation manifests as a forbidden import or a forbidden trait call.

- **Deterministic runtime truth.** "Two runs of the same input produce byte-identical state." Examples: PIE 3-way participant byte-identity (`cross_substrate_determinism::pie_three_participant_round_trip_50_iter`); 1000-tick physics replay; `effective_hash_and_label` recursion. These are testable as soak-loops over recomputed hashes; mechanization is via integration tests, not lints, because the property is observational on output, not structural on source.

- **Observational isolation.** "A subsystem reading state must not perturb that state, must not increment counters on failure paths, must not see different values on two consecutive reads." Examples: ADR-116's increment-only-on-success contract; the editor-ui canary's pure-observational tick (`successful_ticks` increments only on `Ok`); the `validate_handles` post-restore guard. Mechanizable at the trait level (single method contract) but only when a Plugin / participant / extractor abstraction already exists for the contract to attach to.

- **Advisory semantics.** "This number / this metric / this classifier is informational, not authoritative — consumers may not feed it back into the deterministic state machine." Examples: ADR-115 phase-2.5 Tier-C analytical metrics (`max_depth` / `scc_count` / `dependency_diameter`); telemetry counters; the (not-yet-existing) Layer-Inspection classifier. Authority assertion is **negative** — "this thing must NOT influence X" — and negative assertions are far harder to mechanize than positive ones because every new state-machine input becomes a candidate violation site.

- **Architectural layering.** "Layer N may not invalidate Layer N-1." `REACTIVE_INVALIDATION.md` §5.2 no-inversion is the canonical example: gfx may not write cad-graph; Layer 4 may not drive Layer 3. Today partially enforced via `forbidden-dep` (the import direction is structurally blocked) and partially via doctrine review (the API-call direction has no lint — gfx has no callable that mutates `CadGraph`, but proving the absence is harder than proving the import).

- **Semantic contracts.** "If A holds, B must hold; if A is violated, the system must surface diagnostic X." Examples: PLAN §10.4 dogfood rule (Tier-2 plugins must be expressible through the same `Plugin` trait Tier-3 plugins use); failure-class declarations; `MutationOutsideOperation` hard-error. Mechanizable at the lint level (presence-of-declaration) but not always at the type level (the *meaning* of the declaration is doctrine, not type).

- **Unstable experimentation.** "We don't know yet what the right invariant is." Anything in active design pressure: future per-node fine-grained dependency tracking; the eventual `GraphEvent` cross-layer event stream (ADR-115 phase-4); render-snapshot wire format. By definition not graduable today. Graduating an unstable invariant is the classical premature-abstraction failure mode.

The taxonomy intentionally has no single "right" mechanism per row — that is the next section's question. A "structural authority boundary" might graduate to a lint, a runtime-assertion, or stay prose-only depending on cost; the row label suggests the *kind* of authority, not the enforcement choice.

## 4. Enforcement-tier taxonomy

Five tiers of mechanism for asserting an invariant, ordered by strength and (roughly inversely) by ossification cost. Each tier has characteristic strengths and weaknesses; the graduation question (§5) maps an invariant onto a tier, not "is this enforced or not."

- **Executable (compiler / type system / const-eval / proc-macro).** The strongest tier: violation prevents compilation. Strengths: zero runtime cost, IDE-visible, refactor-resistant. Weaknesses: highly ossifying — once a property is at the type level, every consumer is structurally coupled to it; encoding nuance in types is often verbose; proc-macros add build-time cost and complicate IDE behavior. Examples in the workspace: `Plugin` trait's `Send + 'static` bound; `kernel/audit-ledger`'s `EventId` newtype; the `Tolerance::new(t)` constructor refusing non-finite or non-positive values.

- **Linting (architecture lints, clippy, custom AST walkers).** Strong: violation fails CI. Strengths: doesn't ossify the type system; can express cross-crate / cross-module rules; carries an exemptions registry for graduation hedges; per-rule provenance via `tools/architecture-lints/src/<lint>.rs`. Weaknesses: heuristic — the lint's algorithm is narrower than the invariant it stands for (the §6.3 false-authority cost); maintenance debt as the workspace evolves; tests against fixtures, not real code, can drift. Examples: the 9 enforcement architecture lints (forbidden-dep / split-exemption / no-utils / graph-foundation / editor-state-ownership / command-bus / projection-modules / kernel-isolation / failure-class) plus 1 supplementary (snapshot-participate, warning-tier).

- **Runtime assertion (`debug_assert`, contract checks at boundaries).** Medium: violation panics in debug, may silently corrupt in release. Strengths: catches dynamic violations the type system can't; close to where the violation manifests; cheap to add. Weaknesses: doesn't fail CI unless tests exercise the path; release builds may skip; if the assertion's side-effect is observable, it becomes a coupled feature. Examples: cycle detection in `effective_hash_and_label_inner`; `validate_handles` post-restore.

- **Tests (unit / integration / replay / soak / property).** Medium-to-strong, depending on coverage: violation fails CI when the test runs. Strengths: assertion can be arbitrarily expressive; can pin behavior the type system can't; integration tests cover cross-substrate composition. Weaknesses: a passing test only covers what the test exercises; tests pin specific shapes that may need to evolve (the §6.4 ossification cost); `cross_substrate_determinism::pie_three_participant_round_trip_50_iter` pins 50 iterations, not 51 — the boundary is incidental. Examples: 1755 workspace tests across 215 binaries; 4 retroactive `*_plugin_impls_canary_protocol` tests per ADR-116; the cad-projection 1-tick observation test.

- **Social-governance only (code review, doctrine docs, naming conventions).** Weakest mechanically: violation slips through if the reviewer doesn't notice. Strengths: zero ossification cost; preserves design optionality; cheap to update; can express subtleties no lint or type can; naming conventions inherited by example are nearly free. Weaknesses: drifts under contributor turnover; no CI signal; relies on doc discoverability (which itself drifts). Examples: this doctrine; `NON_GOALS.md`; the dispatch-pattern conventions captured in cross-session memory; the `docs/architecture/README.md` navigation hub.

The tiers are not a hierarchy of correctness — they are a hierarchy of **enforcement strength traded against ossification cost**. A correctly-classified invariant at the social-governance tier is a stronger architectural commitment than an incorrectly-classified one at the executable tier, because the latter ossifies the wrong shape.

## 5. Graduation criteria — when prose → executable

Deliberately short and conservative. Graduation is the exception, not the default. An invariant graduates from prose to a stronger tier when ALL of the following hold:

1. **Violation has happened more than once.** A single observed failure is anecdote; two or more is pattern. The audit-6 `rge-` prefix dead-code bug surfaced exactly once before being fixed — one violation isn't grounds for a meta-lint about lint-correctness.

2. **Repair cost exceeds prevention cost.** The cumulative future cost of catching the violation in code review (or via post-hoc audit) exceeds the future cost of maintaining the mechanism plus the consumers it ossifies. This is a cost estimate, not a calculation; the estimate has to be defensible at design review.

3. **A stable substrate with low rename pressure exists.** Codifying against a substrate that may rename / split / merge in the next minor-version bump produces broken codification. The `LayoutNodeId`-rename-pending exemption is the visible workspace state of an invariant whose substrate hasn't stabilized; codifying it earlier would have been wasted effort.

4. **The §6 cost section below is acceptable for this case.** Walk every cost; for each, ask "is this case different enough to make this cost worth paying?" If any cost is unacceptable, the invariant stays at the current tier. This is not optional: graduation that doesn't survive the cost walk is the premature-abstraction failure mode wearing a different hat.

The criteria are judgments, not workflows. There is no "graduation request" form, no "graduation review board," no per-quarter graduation cadence. A graduation is an ADR (or, for trait-level codification of an existing invariant, an inline ADR amendment as ADR-116 amended ADR-114's substrate). The dispatch authoring the ADR works through the four criteria as part of the ADR's *Context* + *Consequences* sections.

The criteria are also **directional** — they describe when prose is allowed to graduate, not when an executable rule must demote to prose. Demotion is a separate question (closer to "we got the codification wrong" than "the invariant changed"); demotion is not in scope for this doctrine.

## 6. Costs of mechanization

The longest section by intent. Each cost is real, has happened or will happen in this workspace, and applies to every graduation candidate. The costs are super-linear in the number of codified invariants — N invariants do not cost N times one invariant; they cost roughly N² because the invariants interact (§6.5). The walk-every-cost rule (§5 criterion 4) is what keeps the workspace's enforcement surface from collapsing under its own weight.

### 6.1 Rigidity

Once an invariant is enforced at the executable tier, the substrate it touches resists evolution. A substrate-level change that would violate the invariant requires either lint amendment (governance trail) or workspace-wide migration (refactor cost). The rigidity is asymmetric — adding the enforcement was one PR; loosening it requires audit, ADR, and reviewer consensus. Worse, the rigidity compounds against design-space exploration: a contributor proposing an architecture that would be cleaner with the rule loosened pays the policy-debate cost on every iteration, which suppresses the proposals before they surface.

Concrete example: `forbidden-dep` rule 4 ("`editor-ui` cannot depend on `physics`, `audio`, or `input` directly") is correct today. If a future architectural decision concludes that editor-ui's input handling should land via direct `input` dependency rather than the current command-bus indirection, rule 4 becomes the obstacle to the new design — not a bug, but a structural friction that the new design must argue against before any of its other merits can be evaluated. The rigidity is the feature; it is also the cost.

### 6.2 Compatibility burden

Every consumer of an enforced invariant inherits its constraints. A trait-level codification (ADR-116's `CanaryPlugin: Plugin`) means every future canary author must conform; a lint-level codification (failure-class declaration) means every future Tier-1+Tier-2 crate author must add the doc-comment; a type-level codification (`#[non_exhaustive]` on enums) means every future match site must include `_ =>`. Each constraint is individually small; the total is the contributor onboarding cost, multiplied by every contributor for the lifetime of the workspace.

Concrete example: failure-class declaration is currently required for every Tier-1+Tier-2 crate. The lint is enforced; 36 crates remain in the rollout-debt exemption pool, each clearing only when its first real implementation lands — even crates whose failure semantics are trivial enough that the declaration adds no information. The cost is the floor of every future first-impl PR; the value is what arrives above the floor. The rollout-debt's persistence (45 of 81 originally cleared, 36 remain) is itself evidence that the floor is real and felt.

### 6.3 False authority

A passing lint creates the illusion of correctness for the broader claim it stands for, even when the lint heuristic is narrower than the doctrine. This is the most insidious cost because it actively *worsens* governance — the green CI light says "the rule is enforced," but the rule's enforcement covers only the cases the heuristic recognizes.

The canonical workspace example: the `forbidden-dep` lint's rules 3-6 lived as dead code for an unknown duration before audit-6 (2026-05-09) caught them. The rules compared workspace package names against bare strings (`"cad-core"`, `"physics"`) when `cargo_metadata::Package::name` returns `rge-`-prefixed names (`"rge-cad-core"`, `"rge-physics"`). Every CI run reported `forbidden-dep PASS` — and four of the six rules were never matching anything. The doctrine was correct; the lint was a green light over silently-disabled enforcement. The fix (per `tools/architecture-lints/src/forbidden_dep.rs:14-22`) is now in place and the lint is real, but the period of false authority is the cost paid; the cost would have repeated for any rule added without prefix-aware testing. The post-audit comment-block at the head of `forbidden_dep.rs` is itself a doctrine artifact, not just a comment — it tells future lint-authors that the `rge-` prefix is load-bearing for correctness.

### 6.4 Test substrate ossification

Tests pin specific shapes that the substrate may need to evolve away from. A test asserting "`pie_three_participant_round_trip_50_iter` produces byte-identical output across 50 iterations" makes the 50-iteration count and the 3-participant composition load-bearing for the test's correctness — even when both are incidental to the invariant the test stands for (which is byte-identity in the limit, not at exactly 50 iterations with exactly 3 participants).

Concrete example: the audit-3 H3 SnapshotParticipate assertion `stateful_tier2_list_does_not_contain_audited_removals` asserts gfx is NOT in `STATEFUL_TIER2_CRATES`. When Phase 6 lands the formal `gfx.render-snapshot` participant, that test must flip its assertion (per `SCENE_EXTRACTION_CONTRACT.md` §6.3). The test is correctly designed as a migration trip-wire (nothing about Phase 6 lands silently), but the cost is real: the test's assertion direction is itself a load-bearing decision that the Phase 6 author must consciously update. A test asserting the *invariant* (cross-substrate composition deterministic) rather than the *current state* (gfx not in list) would not have this cost — but writing such a test was harder, so the workspace has the trip-wire instead.

### 6.5 Enforcement coupling

Once two invariants are enforced via the same mechanism, changing either may require touching the other. This is meta-coupling that prose doesn't have — two prose invariants that happen to live in the same paragraph are independent; two invariants that share a lint module aren't.

Concrete example: the `graph-foundation` lint enforces both Check 1 (no `NodeId`/`EdgeId`/`StableHash` redefinition outside `kernel/graph-foundation`) and Check 2 (no `BTreeMap<K, BTreeSet<K>>` adjacency reinvention). The two checks are *related* (both prevent reinventing graph primitives) but *distinct* (Check 1 is forbidden-name; Check 2 is forbidden-pattern). A future change to Check 1's name list (say, adding `RevisionId` per ADR-115 phase-3) would need to think about whether Check 2's pattern also wants extending — even though the pattern in question (adjacency reinvention) is unrelated to revisions. The coupling is structural to the lint module, not the doctrine.

### 6.6 Abstraction pressure

Three similar invariants invite a fourth plus a generalization layer. The generalization layer's lifecycle then has to be maintained alongside the invariants themselves. This is the failure mode the consolidation phase repeatedly resisted — and Phase 1 reintroduces it under the disguise of "executable governance." The pressure is reflexive: every successful codification creates an example that future codifications can claim similarity to, lowering the apparent cost of each subsequent graduation while the actual cumulative cost rises.

Concrete example: the workspace today has 9 architecture lints. A 10th-lint candidate (per `docs/§18/ARCHITECTURE_LINTS.md` reviewer guidance) faces the question: "is this lint's rationale strong enough that it justifies not just its own complexity, but the eventual pressure for a 'lint-authoring framework' with shared `Violation`-classification, exemption-handling, fixture-runner glue?" The answer today is yes per-lint, no for the framework. Each new lint nudges the framework-pressure higher; at some count (perhaps 12, perhaps 20), the framework pressure becomes dominant and a new agent attempts a generalization. The generalization itself becomes substrate-with-its-own-lifecycle-cost. Resisting the framework while the count grows is itself a senior-engineering act, and the social-governance tier is what holds it.

### 6.7 Future evolution cost

Every codified invariant adds a cost-of-edit term to future architectural decisions. A change in one part of the workspace must be considered against every codified invariant it might touch. With 9 enforcement lints, every PR pays a 9-lint cost; with 18, the cost doubles. The cumulative weight of N invariants is super-linear because the invariants interact (§6.5) and because each one adds a checkbox to the "have we considered…" mental model contributors carry — a cost that compounds across PRs, dispatches, and contributor turnover.

Concrete example: post-Phase-6 the workspace will have a render-snapshot participant lint (per `SCENE_EXTRACTION_CONTRACT.md` §6.3 trip-wire migration) plus presumably a wire-format-stability lint. Both are correct and warranted by the cross-substrate determinism work. Both also mean every future PR that touches gfx pays a 2-lint cost in the "did this break gfx-related governance?" mental model. Multiply across the 10+ subsystems and the cumulative cost is real — not in CI runtime (that's free) but in contributor cognitive overhead, review depth, and the increasing friction of "moving fast in any direction." The cost is the price paid for the invariants' value; the doctrine's role is to ensure the price is paid only when the value justifies it.

### 6.8 Why the cost walk matters

The seven costs above are individually tractable. The reason §5 criterion 4 ("the §6 cost section is acceptable for this case") is non-optional is that the costs interact and compound: a graduation that incurs only "rigidity" plus "compatibility burden" plus "future evolution cost" — three of the seven — has paid 3/7 of the cost catalogue, but the actual cumulative friction is closer to the product than the sum, because each cost amplifies the others through the substrate the invariant touches. A graduation passing the criteria with 6/7 acceptable costs is *worse* than a graduation passing with 3/7, because the 7th cost — the unacceptable one — is what determines whether the graduation will be regretted. **The walk-every-cost rule treats each cost as veto, not vote.**

## 7. Failure modes of over-mechanization

§6 catalogues the costs paid per graduation. This section catalogues the system-level failure modes that emerge when graduations accumulate without restraint — the ways the enforcement *suite* can fail even when each individual rule is justified. Anti-patterns (§10) name what to refuse to build; this section names what the suite collectively becomes if the refusals lapse. The seven modes reinforce each other; once two are present, the third typically follows.

- **Ossified architecture.** Per-substrate rigidity (§6.1) compounds across the suite. Substrate evolution that touches three enforced invariants pays three rigidity-cost terms; substrate evolution touching ten pays ten plus the cross-coupling (§6.5) between them. Past some threshold the substrate stops being able to evolve semantically — only mechanically — and design-space exploration ceases at the doctrine layer rather than the prototype layer. The signal: design-review conversations in which the answer to "could we do X?" is "the lints would need to change first."

- **False confidence.** Green CI as architectural correctness signal. Each lint covers what its heuristic recognizes (§6.3); the suite collectively covers the *union* of those heuristics, not the doctrine. A reviewer trusting the suite reads CI green and stops looking; the gap between heuristic coverage and doctrine coverage becomes the workspace's blind spot. The signal: post-incident analyses in which "this should have been caught" is the framing — the suite was supposed to catch it, but the heuristic didn't extend that far.

- **Enforcement gaming.** Contributors structure code to satisfy the lint rather than express intent. The lint becomes the contract; the doctrine the lint stood for fades. Visible in code that has the right shape (passes the lint) but the wrong rationale (the contributor was steering past the lint, not following the doctrine). The signal: PR diffs whose explanatory comments are about lint compliance rather than design.

- **Test-substrate coupling.** Tests that pin invariants become load-bearing for the substrate, not just for the test suite. A test asserting "X has 5 variants" makes "5" architecturally significant; a refactor adding a 6th must update the test consciously. Multiplied across hundreds of tests, the substrate calcifies into the assertion shape rather than the underlying truth. §6.4 captures the per-test cost; the system-level mode is when the cumulative test surface becomes a *separate* substrate the design must conform to.

- **Framework gravity.** Three lints with similar shape invite a fourth + a generalization layer (§6.6). The generalization layer becomes its own substrate, with its own failure-class declarations, its own exemptions, its own evolution cost. Past some count the framework becomes the dominant artifact and the lints become its consumers. The signal: discussions whose subject is the lint *infrastructure* rather than any specific architectural claim.

- **Accidental public contracts.** Internal invariants graduated to executable form become observable to consumers — workspace clients, plugin authors, reviewers reading the lint suite. What was "the way we currently do it" silently becomes "the way it is required to be done." Future architectural changes must now manage backwards-compatibility for invariants that were never intended as public guarantees. The signal: external requests citing a lint by name as if it were a stability commitment.

- **Inability to evolve semantics safely.** The cumulative weight of enforced invariants makes semantic refactors prohibitive. A change that is correct under every individual rule but conflicts with the system-level shape the rules collectively encode cannot land without coordinated lint-suite revision; the revision-cost dominates the refactor's value. The signal: "we should change X" recurring across multiple sessions without ever crossing the activation threshold.

The seven modes are not independent — they reinforce each other. Ossification feeds framework gravity (the substrate is rigid enough to support a framework); framework gravity feeds false confidence (the framework's existence implies coverage); false confidence feeds enforcement gaming (the contributors' attention shifts to the visible system). The §5 cost walk catches per-graduation contributions; the §10 anti-pattern list refuses substrate that would amplify them. Naming the failure modes here is the third leg: it tells future contributors what to look for when the discipline starts to slip, before any specific decision crosses the line.

## 8. Why selective non-enforcement is healthy

**Lack of mechanization does not imply lack of importance.** This is the single distinction Phase 1 must hold against the pressure that will arrive from Phase 2 (reflection / tooling) and Phase 3 (GPU abstraction): those phases will generate proposals for universal metadata enforcement, generalized authority registries, automated architecture policing, and each proposal will arrive framed as "this important invariant deserves codification." Importance is not the deciding question; the §5 criteria are. Some invariants remain prose precisely because they are contextual, architectural, evolving, judgment-dependent, or expensive to encode safely. The judgment to leave them prose is itself a senior-engineering act, indistinguishable in weight from the judgment to codify others.

Premature enforcement of an evolving invariant produces ossified-but-wrong substrate. The substrate ossifies into the shape the invariant captures *today*; the invariant evolves; the substrate now blocks the evolution it should support. Worse, the substrate has consumers, and the consumers structure their own code against the ossified shape — so the substrate is now load-bearing for code that wouldn't exist if the invariant had stayed prose. The cost of unwinding this state can dwarf the original codification cost by an order of magnitude, because every consumer has to be migrated alongside the substrate change.

Prose holds the truth at lower cost while the substrate is still finding its shape. A doctrine doc can be amended in one PR; an enforced lint with a 36-crate exemption pool cannot. Code review with a doctrine reference (`per REACTIVE_INVALIDATION.md §4.4`) is a cheap, evolvable check that scales with the team's discipline rather than against the substrate's calcification. The reviewer surface is also where genuine architectural insight surfaces — a reviewer noticing that an invariant has been re-articulated in three different ways across three PRs is information the lint suite cannot produce.

Some truths never stabilize enough to mechanize. Naming conventions inherited by example (the `cad-projection.brep-handles` ParticipantId form), engineering taste (when to split vs extend a crate), phase-boundary discipline (the consolidation→EG→reflection→GPU sequencing) — these aren't "not yet ready for codification." They are categorically prose-only. Attempting to codify them produces broken codification that papers over the actual mechanism (judgment) with the wrong mechanism (rules).

The non-enforcement decision is also a **doctrinal commitment**: it tells future contributors that the workspace has deliberately chosen to rely on prose + review for this particular truth. That commitment is itself enforced by example — every doctrine doc that articulates an unenforced invariant raises the cost of opportunistic codification later. The 4 doctrine docs in `docs/architecture/` (after this doc lands) collectively represent that commitment; they are the workspace's most expensive form of governance precisely because they require continued prose maintenance to remain accurate, with no CI signal to detect drift.

## 9. Doctrine that should remain prose by design

Five workspace examples that should likely never graduate to executable form, with reasoning. The list is illustrative, not exhaustive; the point is that significant architectural commitments belong here permanently.

- **Engineering style and design taste.** "When to split a crate vs extend an existing one" is a §1.10.4-style judgment with a rough threshold (≥1000-line files trip `split-exemption`; ~5000-line crates trip merge consideration per PLAN §1.10.3) but the actual decision is taste-driven: cohesion, expected churn, consumer surface area, refactor cost. A lint that codifies this would be worse than the judgment; the existing line-cap lint is the structural floor, not the doctrine.

- **Naming conventions inherited by example.** `cad-projection.brep-handles` ParticipantId follows a `<crate>.<feature>` shape; the convention is captured nowhere structurally — it lives in the example of cad-projection plus the (unenforced) social expectation that future participants will mirror it. Codifying as a regex would catch typos but would also reject any future deliberate departure (e.g. a future participant whose semantic identity is genuinely cross-crate). The convention is inherited because reading three existing participants is enough to understand it; the prose tier is the right tier.

- **"Source-truth wins when prose conflicts with audit-x findings" decisions.** The §18-pack honesty discipline (every pack surfaces 4-6 source/spec inconsistencies) institutionalizes a workflow: when prose says X but the source says Y, the prose is wrong. Codifying this as a lint would require a lint that compares prose to source — categorically unbuildable. Codifying it as a process would be bureaucracy. It works as prose because senior reviewers read both; the workflow is sustained by the §18-pack pattern itself.

- **Phase-boundary discipline (the mode-shifts at consolidation → EG → reflection → GPU).** The current workspace state is mid-Phase-1; the cross-session memory pin (`rge_next_phase_roadmap.md`) holds the binding sequencing. A "phase-boundary lint" would have to encode "what counts as Phase-1 work vs Phase-2 work" — a question with no syntactic answer, because the same code change is in-scope or out-of-scope depending on the dispatch's intent. The discipline lives in the dispatch brief + cross-review pattern, mechanized via the dispatch-pattern conventions (bounded scope + MAY/MUST-NOT file lists), not via the lint suite. The mode-shift is itself part of the discipline — see the brief's framing for this very dispatch.

- **Dispatch-pattern conventions (bounded scope, MAY/MUST-NOT file lists, report-back templates).** The cross-session memory pin (`rge_dispatch_pattern.md`) captures a working pattern that has scaled across ~50 dispatches. The pattern is itself the mechanism; codifying it as a lint over dispatch briefs would require parsing dispatch briefs, would catch only well-formed violations, and would foreclose the deliberate departures that occasionally produce the strongest dispatches (the audit-6 `rge-` prefix fix landed via a deep-audit pattern that didn't follow the standard MAY/MUST-NOT shape). The convention is honored by example, reviewed by the orchestrator, and renegotiated as the workspace's needs evolve. Prose tier; permanent.

- **Cross-review judgment.** The 10 archived ChatGPT cross-reviews captured in `change.md` 2026-05-10 represent a kind of architectural review that no lint can substitute for: external eyes catching the failure modes the workspace's own discipline cannot see (the §0.6 freeze policy holding; the runtime-vs-analytical separation needing formalization; the editor-ui canary's hidden-success of "boundary correctness"). The cross-review pattern is governance that operates above the codification tier; its outputs sometimes graduate to codified form (ADR-115 phase-2.5 amendment; ADR-116) but the pattern itself stays prose because its value is the open-endedness of "what should we be looking at that we aren't."

## 10. Anti-patterns

What this phase MUST NOT build. Each is named because each has been considered (or attempted) somewhere in the project's reasoning history or in the surrounding reference space; each fails for a specific reason that distinguishes it from the legitimate codification work. Naming each anti-pattern explicitly is itself a Phase-1 governance act: future contributors arriving with the anti-pattern's idea see it on the prohibited list before they invest in the design.

- **Universal invariant frameworks.** A framework that classifies any invariant by its tier and emits enforcement of the right kind would be a meta-framework over the 9 lints. Fails because the 9 lints' shared infrastructure (`Violation`, `LintReport`, `Exemptions`, cargo-metadata helpers) is already the right amount of shared infrastructure; further generalization extracts what is unique per lint into a less-readable shape, increases test surface, and ossifies the lint-authoring workflow against an abstraction the next lint may not fit.

- **Governance registries.** A central `tools/governance-registry` listing every workspace invariant with its tier and enforcement-status. Fails because the canonical place for every invariant is the doctrine doc / ADR / lint module that owns it; a parallel registry duplicates the truth and immediately drifts. The cross-review-#9 "Substrate Governance Index" mention (archived in `change.md`) explicitly defers to "if the project becomes large enough" — Phase 1 is not large enough, and this anti-pattern entry pins that.

- **Meta-test abstractions.** A `meta_test::assert_invariant!(name, ...)` macro consolidating the assertion shapes used across the 1755 workspace tests. Fails because the variation in assertion shape *is* the information — a test's assertion is the contract it pins; flattening into a macro erases per-test rationale and adds an indirection the next reader must unwind to understand what the test actually pins.

- **Reflection registries.** A list of every invariant queryable at runtime via reflection. Fails because reflection is a Phase 2 concern (per the binding sequencing in `rge_next_phase_roadmap.md`) and any reflection registry built in Phase 1 will ossify against assumptions the Phase 2 design hasn't yet made. **Explicitly defer.** Building this in Phase 1 is the canonical mode-shift failure.

- **Architecture DSLs.** A workspace DSL for expressing dependency-direction rules / participant contracts / failure-class declarations. Fails because TOML (`exemptions.toml`) plus Rust (the lint modules) plus prose (the doctrine docs) are already the DSL; a new layer adds parsing, error-reporting, IDE-integration, and maintenance for a structure the existing tools express adequately. Every DSL eventually needs a second DSL to talk about it.

- **Generalized lifecycle machinery.** A trait hierarchy or framework for "objects with a lifecycle that needs governance." Fails because the workspace has exactly one such hierarchy today (`Plugin` / `CanaryPlugin`), and ADR-116 deliberately kept it minimal — single trait, single method, no structured telemetry. A second hierarchy without two examples to generalize over invents shape no real consumer needs.

- **"Invariant graduation workflows" with stages / states.** A formalization of §5's criteria into a stateful workflow ("draft → reviewed → accepted → enforced"). Fails because the criteria are judgments, not states; a stateful workflow turns judgment into process, which is bureaucracy by another name. The graduation lives in the ADR that lands the codification; that is workflow enough. A workflow that adds review-board stages or per-tier checklists explicitly violates the "the criteria are judgments" framing in §5.

## 11. Round-9 deferred items as classification probes

Five candidates that surfaced during the consolidation phase and were deferred. This section frames each as **"here is why this candidate belongs in a particular row of the §3 taxonomy"**, NOT "here is what we should implement." The probes are how the taxonomy gets exercised against real workspace pressure; they are not a backlog.

- **REACTIVE_INVALIDATION.md §4 no-inversion invariant.** Layer-4 must not mutate Layer-1/2/3 state. Classification: **architectural layering.** The import-direction half is already structurally enforced via `forbidden-dep` (gfx cannot import cad-core). The API-call-direction half is currently implicit — gfx has no callable that mutates `CadGraph`, but proving the absence is harder than proving the import. A direct test pinning "gfx exposes no `CadGraph`-mutating API" would catch the negative property; the question is whether the test pins additional value (catches a regression) or just creates ossification (the test passes today and the negative property isn't a likely-regressing one). Recommendation per cost walk: low rigidity cost + low compatibility cost + high false-authority risk (the test name implies stronger coverage than it provides) = stay-prose; the structural layering is documented and the import-direction enforcement carries the load.

- **PLAN §10.4 mixed Tier-2 + Tier-3 dogfood test.** Dogfood rule says Tier-2 plugins use the same `Plugin` trait as Tier-3. Classification: **semantic contract.** A canary-tier dogfood smoke test (a single `dogfood_smoke.rs` exercising one Tier-2 + one Tier-3 mock plugin through the same `Plugin` trait surface) is insufficient as semantic-contract evidence because the contract's content is "the same trait *suffices* for both tiers" — a claim about expressiveness, not behavior. Two mock plugins through the same trait don't prove expressiveness; the actual evidence is the 5 production canaries (cad-projection / gfx / physics / audio / editor-ui) compiling against the trait. Recommendation per cost walk: the production canaries already discharge the contract; an additional smoke test pins toy mocks at the cost of test-substrate ossification (§6.4). Stay-prose; the production canary count IS the contract evidence.

- **`successful_ticks()` failure-path test naming uniformity.** The H5 closure landed `successful_ticks` as a uniform trait method; cad-projection and editor-ui carry dedicated `*_unchanged_on_contract_violation` tests; gfx, physics, audio rely on incidental asserts inside their main canary tests. Classification: **observational isolation.** The gold-standard pattern (cad-projection + editor-ui) explicitly tests that the counter doesn't increment on contract violation paths, separate from the success path. The non-gold pattern (gfx/physics/audio) entangles success-path and failure-path assertions in single tests, which works but loses the "the failure path is its own contract" framing. Recommendation per cost walk: uniformity has real value (per-canary trip-wire on the increment-only-on-success invariant) but pinning all 5 to the same shape ossifies the test-name convention — a test renamed at any canary site triggers a workspace-wide convention update. Mid-tier candidate; if pursued, frame as "per-canary trip-wire test" not as "uniform test name."

- **`PanickingTickPlugin` 5x test-fixture duplication.** Each of the 5 canary `plugin_adapter_smoke.rs` files defines its own `PanickingTickPlugin` struct (verified: 5 struct definitions across the 5 files; 32 total references). Classification: **NOT sub-component 1 territory.** This is governance-test substrate (sub-component 3 territory per Phase 1's broader scope) and is deferred within Phase 1, not because the duplication is fine, but because the right deduplication target — adding a public `PanickingTickPlugin`-or-equivalent test helper to `kernel/plugin-host` — would expand plugin-host's public API surface to support testing patterns that currently live in consumer crates. Touching plugin-host's public surface is a design decision (what does plugin-host export to consumers, and at what stability tier?), not opportunistic deduplication. The 5x duplication is the cost of plugin-host *not* yet making this design choice; making it now would be premature codification of a still-stabilizing test substrate.

- **ADR-115 amendment "Layer-Inspection classifier" Followup.** A future classifier that distinguishes Tier-A / Tier-B / Tier-C metric calls at the call site. Classification: **advisory semantics, prose only.** The classifier may be valuable as architectural reasoning (it forces the Tier-A vs Tier-C distinction to be made explicit per metric) but **NOT as enforced contract** because the negative-assertion shape ("Tier-C must not feed back into the deterministic state machine") is not enforceable by a forward-looking lint without a reflection-tier substrate that doesn't exist (and per Phase 2 binding sequencing, won't exist for a while). The ADR-115 phase-2.5 amendment doctrine carries the load today; a classifier — if it ever surfaces — should ship as documentation tooling (advisory, surfacing classification at review time), not enforcement tooling.

Three additional workspace-state probes sharpen the asymmetry argument:

- **The 6 `io-*` manifest `package.metadata.rge.formats` warnings.** Currently warning-level supplementary lint; each `io-*` crate is an empty stub. Classification: **warning-tier as legitimate steady state.** Promoting these to errors today would block 6 stub crates from declaring their format-handler intent, foreclosing the design-space exploration the stubs exist to preserve. Reverting to prose would lose the structural floor that signals "this `io-*` declaration is canonical." The warning-tier is the right tier precisely because it splits the difference: enforcement-shape exists, but failure is non-blocking; the eventual graduation (when the first `io-*` ships a real impl) is paid for then, not now. The deeper point: warning-tier is **not necessarily transitional**. A non-trivial fraction of architectural intent is correctly held at the warning level *indefinitely* — the enforcement-shape carries doctrinal weight (the warning text states the rule), the non-blocking semantics preserve the substrate's design optionality, and the eventual escalation may never come if the design pressure that would justify it never materializes. "Warning indefinitely" is a legitimate steady state for invariants whose enforcement value is real but whose escalation cost is not yet — and may never become — warranted. The io-* warnings inhabit that steady state today; they may inhabit it for the lifetime of the workspace, and that would be correct.

- **The 1 substantive lint exemption (`LayoutNodeId` rename pending).** A single exempted file: `crates/editor-ui/src/layout/node.rs` carrying a string-based reconciler `NodeId` that lints as a `kernel/graph-foundation::NodeId` redefinition. Classification: **graduation hedge.** An exemption with a reason field + follow-up plan is not a graduation failure — it is the doctrine acknowledging that the lint heuristic is narrower than the rule (per §6.3) and the false-positive cost is being paid temporarily until the rename closes the gap. The exemption is the workspace's correct answer to "the lint is right in spirit but wrong on this file"; replacing it with looser lint heuristics would broaden the false-authority surface for every other file the lint touches.

- **Failure-class rollout debt (36 crates exempted).** The `failure-class` lint is enforced workspace-wide; 36 crates carry exemption entries because their `lib.rs` is verbatim `//! \`rge-<name>\` — stub crate.` and adding the declaration before any real implementation would pin a failure semantics that the eventual implementation might not match. Classification: **partial enforcement as transitional equilibrium.** This is not "the lint is half-broken"; this is "the lint enforces what is determinable today, and explicit exemptions track what isn't." A lint with no exemptions and a 100% rollout would have required pre-committing 36 failure-class declarations against unwritten code — broken codification by another name. The exemption pool *is* the graduation lifecycle, made visible.

> *These are classification probes, not implementation tasks. Selection per the taxonomy is the next session's job, if any.*

## 12. What this doctrine does NOT do

Explicit out-of-scope statement, in the same shape as `NON_GOALS.md`'s scope-boundary framing.

- **NOT Phase 2 reflection / tooling.** Reflection registries, runtime-queryable invariants, observability frameworks — all defer per the binding sequencing in `rge_next_phase_roadmap.md`. This doctrine names reflection as an anti-pattern in Phase 1 (§10) precisely to prevent the bleed.
- **NOT Phase 3 GPU abstraction.** Render-graph ownership, snapshot/render separation, replay compatibility for GPU resources — Phase 3 work; not in scope.
- **NOT a binding ADR commitment on any single invariant.** The §11 probes are classification reasoning, not graduation decisions. A future ADR may codify any of them or none; this doctrine does not pre-commit.
- **NOT an operational backlog.** The §11 list is not a TODO. Selection (if any) is a separate dispatch's call.
- **NOT a generalized framework.** The taxonomy is reasoning, not machinery. The §4 enforcement tiers describe what already exists; the §3 invariant classes describe distinctions already implicit. Neither becomes a runtime structure, a registry, or a meta-trait.
